About this framework

Career Progression Framework

A single framework for the Technology and Business tracks across five domains. It defines what good looks like at every level — from someone just starting out to someone shaping the direction of the organisation. What changes between tracks is the depth and scope expected at each level.

How it works

Framework structure

Understanding how the framework is structured helps you navigate each section and make sense of what is expected at each level.

01 Domain

The framework is organised into five capability areas that cover how people work, deliver, build relationships, grow their influence, and contribute to the broader direction of the organisation. Each domain applies to both the Technology and Business tracks — the expected depth varies by level and track.

Technical
Applies technical capability to build, improve, and maintain effective solutions.
Execution
Drives reliable delivery through planning, prioritization, and self-management.
Collaboration
Builds effective working relationships through communication, feedback, and teamwork.
Leadership
Creates direction, supports teams, and helps others grow.
Strategic
Connects work to broader business, product, and organisational goals.

02 Competency

Within each domain, competencies group related skills and behaviours that serve a shared purpose. They provide the mid-level structure between the broad capability area and the specific observable behaviours used for assessment.

Code Quality & Testing Groups skills around writing, testing, and maintaining high-quality code.
Decision Making Groups skills around reasoning clearly, evaluating trade-offs, and owning outcomes.
Communication & Knowledge Sharing Groups skills around expressing ideas clearly and helping others build context.
··· And more competencies across all five domains.

03 Criteria

Criteria are the specific, observable behaviours that define what a competency looks like in practice. Each criteria is assessed independently, allowing for a precise and honest picture of where someone stands across different aspects of the same skill area.

Writing Code Produces code that is readable, maintainable, and handles edge cases appropriately.
Mentoring Supports the growth of others through guidance, feedback, and shared experience.
Work Breakdown Breaks work into clear, manageable steps that can be tracked and delivered incrementally.
··· And more criteria within each competency.
↓
04 Proficiency

Each criteria is rated on a six-level scale that reflects how independently and consistently a behaviour is demonstrated. The scale is the same across all criteria and both tracks, making it possible to compare profiles and identify growth areas in a structured way.

Novice Has no knowledge of the subject.
Foundation Understands what it is and why it matters.
Working Applies it with some support when needed.
Independent Applies it fully without needing guidance.
Expert Sets the standard and develops others across the organisation.
Specialist Has set standards across multiple organisations at industry level.
↓
05 Evidence

At higher proficiency levels, a claim of capability should be grounded in something concrete. Evidence serves as a reference point that makes the assessment more credible and actionable — both for the person filling it in and for anyone reviewing it.

Project A specific deliverable or initiative where the skill was applied and produced a concrete outcome.
Training A course, workshop, or structured learning programme completed to develop this capability.
Peer Review Validation from a colleague, manager, or collaborator who observed the behaviour directly.
Experience Previous work or responsibilities that demonstrate the capability in a real context.
Certificate A formal credential issued by a recognised institution confirming the level of knowledge or skill.
↓
06 Career Levels

Each track has five career levels that reflect increasing scope, autonomy, and impact. Proficiency benchmarks define the minimum expected score per domain at each level. Both tracks share the same five-step progression — what changes is the weighting across domains.

Junior Building foundational skills with close guidance. Learning the craft and the context.
Mid Delivering independently on routine work. Developing depth in key areas.
Senior Operating autonomously across complex work. Starting to elevate others.
Lead Leading across teams. Shaping direction and enabling others to deliver.
Principal Defining standards and strategy at an organisational level. Operating beyond the immediate team.

Compensation

Salary bands within each level

Each career level has three salary bands — I, II, and III — that reflect how far someone has progressed within that level. Band III signals readiness for a promotion conversation, but the final decision requires a formal review. Moving to the next level is a promotion, not an automatic outcome of meeting the proficiency benchmarks.

I Learning

The person meets the minimum domain requirements for their current level. No domain score exceeds the minimum — they are establishing the foundation of the role.

No domain exceeds the current level minimum
II Established

Some domain scores exceed the current level minimum. The person is demonstrating growth within the role and building capability beyond what is expected.

Some domains exceed the current level minimum
III Mastering

Most domain scores exceed the current level minimum. The person is consistently performing above the role expectations and is ready for a promotion conversation.

All growth domains meet the next level minimum

How the process works

A structured 12-month cycle connecting self-assessment, leadership input, goal setting, and continuous development. The cycle repeats annually, with a check-in at the 6-month mark.

Career review & new cycle

Revisit the framework, align on updates, and set the stage for a new assessment cycle.

Self-assessment

Rate yourself on each criteria at your current level of operation. Evidence required for Independent and Expert ratings.

Leader assessment

Leader completes the same assessment independently to surface blind spots and prepare for an evidence-based conversation.

Individual Development Plan meeting

Compare assessments, align on gaps, and co-build a signed Individual Development Plan with clear goals and milestones.

6-month checklist

Structured mid-point check-in to review progress, assess milestones, and update the plan where needed.

12-month review

Full cycle close — recognise achievements, document gaps, and conclude the plan before starting a new cycle.

Where this framework comes from

The Manager’s Path Camille Fournier
The Manager’s Path
Camille Fournier · O’Reilly, 2017

This framework is grounded in The Manager’s Path by Camille Fournier — a practical guide that maps each stage of the technical leadership journey, from engineer to CTO. It defines what good looks like at every level and remains one of the most widely referenced books on engineering management in the industry.

Many technology companies have used the book as a foundation for their own career frameworks. Spotify, Rent the Runway, CircleCI, and Dropbox are among those that published engineering ladders and competency matrices that trace back to the same thinking. Rent the Runway’s multi-track ladder served as a direct structural reference when shaping this framework.
